# How the Chase 5/24 Rule Works in 2026
====================================

Are you a credit card enthusiast trying to maximize your travel rewards, or perhaps you're looking to sign up for a new Chase credit card? In either case, you're likely interested in understanding the 5/24 rule and how it affects your ability to apply for new Chase credit cards. Simply put, the 5/24 rule states that Chase won't approve your application if you've opened more than five new credit accounts in the past 24 months.

## Understanding the 5/24 Rule and Why It Exists

While this rule may seem restrictive, it's primarily designed to prevent individuals from over-extending themselves financially. By applying for too many credit cards in a short period, credit scores can take a hit due to increased credit utilization, and it also raises the risk of individuals not being able to pay back the borrowed funds. This, in turn, poses a significant risk to both the borrower and the lender.

### The Implications of the 5/24 Rule

When you apply for a Chase credit card, the company evaluates your creditworthiness based on multiple factors, including credit scores, payment history, income, and debt-to-income ratio. If you've exceeded the 5/24 limit, Chase may decline your application, even if you have a good credit history.

### The 5/24 Count: A Detailed Explanation

The 5/24 rule specifically counts the following types of credit accounts:

* Credit card applications through Chase's direct channels
* Credit card applications through authorized third-party vendors, such as credit card matchmakers
* Student and business credit cards, including those through Chase's business platform

However, it does not count the following:

* Retail Cards (e.g., store credit cards)
* Payday Loans or Other Short-Term Loans
* Credit Card Applications that are subsequently cancelled or closed

## Navigating the 5/24 Rule to Your Advantage

While you can't directly game the system, being aware of how the 5/24 rule works will help you plan your credit card strategy more effectively. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

### Understanding When to Apply

If you've recently opened a few credit cards or are close to reaching the 5/24 limit, it might be wise to wait before applying for another Chase card. This ensures you're not rejected and have more time to plan your applications strategically.

### Picking the Right Chase Card

Not all Chase cards are created equal, and some are more valuable than others. Focus on cards that offer rewards and benefits that align with your spending habits and financial goals.

### Alternative Solutions

If you need a new credit card or are looking for additional rewards, don't be discouraged if you're close to the 5/24 limit. Explore other options from rival credit card issuers or consider canceling a credit card with a lower reward earning rate.

## Tips to Keep in Mind

* **Be mindful of your credit card applications**: Avoid applying for multiple cards in quick succession.
* **Keep track of your accounts**: Monitor your credit history for new account openings.
* **Plan ahead**: Wait until you're no longer subject to the 5/24 rule to apply for Chase credit cards.

## FAQ

### 1. What is the 5/24 rule?

The 5/24 rule states that Chase won't approve your application if you've opened more than five new credit accounts in the past 24 months.

### 2. Does the Chase 5/24 rule apply to retail credit cards and payday loans?

No, the 5/24 rule specifically excludes retail credit cards, payday loans, and other short-term loans.

### 3. Can I still apply for a Chase credit card if I'm close to the 5/24 limit?

While it's not guaranteed, you can still apply for a Chase credit card if you're close to the 5/24 limit. However, the card issuer may scrutinize your application more closely.

### 4. Is the 5/24 rule a fixed rule or can it change over time?

While the 5/24 rule is in place, its future modifications or possible elimination cannot be predicted with certainty. Check for updates or announcements from Chase before applying for a credit card.
