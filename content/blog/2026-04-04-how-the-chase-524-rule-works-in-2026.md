# How the Chase 5/24 Rule Works in 2026

If you're planning to apply for a Chase credit card, chances are you've heard of the 5/24 (or 5/25 or 5/28) rule. Simply put, the rule states that if you've opened five or more new credit card accounts in the past 24 (or 25 or 28) months, your application for a new Chase credit card will likely be rejected.

However, understanding the 5/24 rule is only half the battle. In this post, we'll break down how it works, what you need to know, and how you can still earn rewards and sign-up bonuses with Chase credit cards despite this rule.

## What is the 5/24 Rule?

The 5/24 rule is a credit card policy enforced by Chase that determines whether or not to approve a new credit card application. The rule looks at the number of new credit card accounts you've opened over the previous 24 months. If you've opened five or more new accounts, your application is likely to be rejected.

However, the rule is not set in stone, and there are factors that can influence a card issuer's decision to approve or deny an application. For example:

*   Some Chase credit cards are exempt from the 5/24 rule, such as business credit cards or cards that do not report to your personal credit.
*   If you've recently closed a credit card account, it may be treated as a new account and count towards the total.
*   If you have other credit cards with Chase that have been closed for more than two years, they may not be included in the count.

## History of the 5/24 Rule

The 5/24 rule has its roots in the early 2010s, when credit card issuers began to tighten their policies in response to regulatory changes. Chase, in particular, was at the forefront of credit card issuers in implementing this rule.

## How Does the 5/24 Rule Affect Your Credit Score?

The 5/24 rule does not directly affect your credit score, but it can have an indirect impact. Here are some ways in which it can influence your credit score:

*   When you open multiple credit cards in a short period, it can trigger a credit inquiry, which can temporarily lower your credit score.
*   If you're unable to get approved for a credit card, you may not be able to build credit or consolidate debt through credit cards, which can impact your overall credit score.

## Exceptions to the 5/24 Rule

While the 5/24 rule is widely enforced, there are some exceptions to the rule. Here are some examples:

*   **Business Credit Cards**: Many Chase business credit cards are exempt from the 5/24 rule. This is because business credit cards are treated as separate from personal credit, and card issuers do not report them to your personal credit.
*   **Credit Cards Exempt from the 5/24 Rule**: Some Chase credit cards, such as the Chase Sapphire Preferred Card, are exempt from the 5/24 rule. This is because these cards have longer credit histories and are seen as lower-risk by card issuers.
*   **Credit Cards with No Annual Fee**: Chase credit cards with no annual fee are often exempt from the 5/24 rule. This is because these cards are seen as lower-risk by card issuers, and the lack of an annual fee means that cardholders are less likely to default on payments.
